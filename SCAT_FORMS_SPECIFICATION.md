### Page 3: Steps 1-5 Details

**Box 1: Red Flags** (Red box):
- Neck pain or tenderness
- Seizure or convulsion
- Double vision
- Loss of consciousness
- Weakness or tingling/burning in more than 1 arm or in the legs
- Deteriorating conscious state
- Vomiting
- Severe or increasing headache
- Increasingly restless, agitated or combative
- GCS <15
- Visible deformity of the skull

**Step 1: Observable Signs** (Witnessed / Observed on Video):
- Lying motionless on playing surface
- Falling unprotected to the surface
- Balance/gait difficulties
- Disorientation or confusion
- Blank or vacant look
- Facial injury after head trauma
- Impact seizure
- High-risk mechanism of injury

**Step 2: Glasgow Coma Scale**
- Time of Assessment, Date of Assessment
- Best Eye Response (1-4)
- Best Verbal Response (1-5)
- Best Motor Response (1-6)
- Glasgow Coma Score (E + V + M)
- Three columns for repeated assessments

**Step 3: Cervical Spine Assessment**
- Does athlete report neck pain at rest? Y/N
- Is there tenderness to palpation? Y/N
- Full range of ACTIVE pain free movement? Y/N
- Limb strength and sensation normal? Y/N

**Step 4: Coordination & Ocular/Motor Screen**
- Finger-to-nose normal? Y/N
- Can look side-to-side and up-and-down without double vision? Y/N
- Observed extraocular eye movements normal? Y/N

**Step 5: Memory Assessment (Maddocks Questions)** - 1 point each:

**Return-to-Learn (RTL) Strategy** (Green header)

Facilitating RTL is a vital part of the recovery process for student-athletes. HCPs should work with stakeholders on education and school policies to facilitate academic support. Not all athletes will need a RTL strategy or academic support. If symptoms exacerbate during cognitive activity or screen time, or if there is a history of learning concerns, consider implementing a RTL strategy at diagnosis. Progression should be symptom limited.

| Step | Mental Activity | Activity at Each Step | Goal |
|------|-----------------|----------------------|------|
| 1 | Daily activities that do not result in more than a mild exacerbation* of symptoms related to the current concussion. | Typical activities during the day (e.g., reading) while minimizing screen time. Start with 5â€“15 min at a time and increase gradually. | Gradual return to typical activities. |
| 2 | School activities. | Homework, reading, or other cognitive activities outside of the classroom. | Increase tolerance to cognitive work. |
| 3 | Return to school part time. | Gradual introduction of schoolwork. May need to start with a partial school day or with greater access to rest breaks during the day. | Increase academic activities. |
| 4 | Return to school full time. | Gradually progress school activities until a full day can be tolerated without more than mild* symptom exacerbation. | Return to full academic activities and catch up on missed work. |

**NOTE:** Following an initial period of relative rest (24-48 hours following injury at Step 1), athletes can begin a gradual and incremental increase in their cognitive load. Progression through the strategy for students should be slowed when there is more than a mild and brief symptom exacerbation.

*Mild and brief exacerbation of symptoms is defined as an increase of no more than 2 points on a 0-10 point scale (with 0 representing no symptoms and 10 the worst symptoms imaginable) for less than an hour when compared with the baseline value reported prior to cognitive activity.

### Page 15: RTS Strategy

**Return-to-Sport (RTS) Strategy** (Green header)

Return to sport participation after an SRC follows a graduated stepwise strategy. RTS occurs in conjunction with and under the supervision of an HCP. Following an initial period of relative rest, clinicians can implement Step 2, light (Step 2A) and then moderate (Step 2B) aerobic activity; of the RTS strategy as a treatment for acute concussion. The athlete should not advance to later stages of the RTS strategy by symptoms, cognitive function, clinical findings, and clinical judgement. Differentiating early activity (step 1), aerobic exercise (Step 2), and individual sport-specific exercise (Step 3) as part of the progression of Steps 2-4 may be useful for the athlete and their support network. Medical determination of readiness, including psychological readiness, to return to at-risk activities should occur prior to returning to any activities at risk of contact, collision or fall. Athletes may be moved into the later stages that involve full or part contact (Steps 4-6 and Step 5) if there are no new risk of head impact. Unrestricted RTS should only occur when the athlete has successfully navigated to and in keeping with local laws/requirements.

| Step | Exercise Strategy | Activity at Each Step | Goal |
|------|------------------|----------------------|------|
| 1 | Symptom-limited activity. | Daily activities that do not exacerbate symptoms (e.g., walking). | Gradual reintroduction of work/school. |
| 2 | Aerobic exercise: **2A â€“ Light** (up to approx. 55% max HR) **then 2B â€“ Moderate** (up to approximately 70% max HR) | Stationary cycling or walking at slow to medium pace. May start light resistance training that does not result in more than mild and brief exacerbation* of concussion symptoms. | Increase heart rate. |
| 3 | Individual sport-specific exercise. **NOTE:** If sport-specific exercise involves any risk of head impact, medical determination of readiness to RTS should occur prior to step 3. | Sport-specific training away from the team environment (e.g., running, change of direction and/or individual training drills away from the team environment). No activities at risk of head impact. | Add movement, change of direction. |

**Steps 4-6 should begin after resolution of any symptoms, abnormalities in cognitive function, and any other clinical findings related to the current concussion, including with and after physical exertion.**

| Step | Exercise Strategy | Activity at Each Step | Goal |
|------|------------------|----------------------|------|
| 4 | Non-contact training drills. | Exercise to high intensity including more challenging training drills (e.g., passing drills, multiplayer training). Can integrate into team environment. | Resume usual intensity of exercise, coordination, and increased thinking. |
| 5 | Full contact practice. | Participate in normal training activities. | Restore confidence and assess functional skills by coaching staff. |
| 6 | Return to sport. | Normal game play. | |

## Critical Calculations

### SCAT6 & SCOAT6 Auto-Calculations:
1. **Symptom Number**: Count of symptoms rated > 0 (max 22)
2. **Symptom Severity Score**: Sum of all symptom ratings (max 132 for 22 symptoms Ã— 6)
3. **Orientation Score**: Sum of correct answers (max 5)
4. **Immediate Memory Score**: Sum of all 3 trials (max 30)
5. **Digits Backwards Score**: Count of completed digit strings (max 4)
6. **Months in Reverse Score**: 1 if completed in <30 seconds with 0 errors, else 0
7. **Concentration Score**: Digits + Months (max 5)
8. **mBESS Total Errors**: Sum of Double Leg + Tandem + Single Leg (max 30)
9. **Delayed Recall Score**: Count of correctly recalled words (max 10)
10. **Total Cognitive Score**: Orientation + Immediate Memory + Concentration + Delayed Recall (max 50)
11. **Tandem Gait Average**: (Trial 1 + Trial 2 + Trial 3) / 3
12. **Tandem Gait Fastest**: MIN(Trial 1, Trial 2, Trial 3)
13. **Glasgow Coma Scale**: Eye + Verbal + Motor (max 15)
14. **Anxiety Screen (GAD-7)**: Sum of 7 questions (max 21)
15. **Depression Screen (PHQ-2)**: Sum of 2 questions (max 6)
16. **Sleep Screen**: Sum of 5 questions (max 17)
17. **Dual Task Cognitive Accuracy**: Number Correct / Number Attempted
